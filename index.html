South Texas Yard Notes

San Antonio lawns, from the aquifer's point of view.

Every San Antonio yard decision eventually runs through the Edwards Aquifer. The city drinks from it, SAWS meters it, and when its level at the J-17 well drops, the whole metro's sprinkler schedule tightens with it. Add USDA zone 9a heat, soils that flip from Hill Country caliche to blackland clay within a few miles, and ancient live oaks with their own disease politics, and lawn care here is genuinely local knowledge.

Know your drought stage before your grass type

SAWS drought restrictions move in stages tied to aquifer levels — each stage narrows watering to specific days and hours by address. It changes with the rains, so the only reliable habit is checking the SAWS drought-restrictions page before programming anything. Hand watering stays legal in every stage, which is how you keep young trees and beds alive in a deep Stage 3 summer.

Two soils, two playbooks

North and west of Loop 1604 you are often on a few inches of soil over caliche — alkaline, fast-draining, iron chlorosis territory. South and east sits blackland clay: fertile but slow to drain and brutal when compacted. Compost topdressing helps both, for opposite reasons. If a shovel test hits white rock at six inches, plant like the Hill Country; if it peels up in dense ribbons, water like clay — slowly, in cycles.

Turf that earns its water

  • Bermuda — the drought survivor; full sun only.
  • St. Augustine — the shade answer, and the first grass to suffer in restrictions; raise the mower to 4 inches in July.
  • Zoysia — middle path on water, dense enough to fight weeds on its own.
  • Buffalograss — native, nearly waterless, best on unirrigated sun.

The live-oak rule

Oak wilt spreads through fresh pruning wounds and root grafts, and South Texas is its heartland. Prune oaks only in the coldest or hottest weeks (roughly December–January or July–August), paint every cut immediately, and never move unseasoned oak firewood. No lawn program matters more than protecting a 200-year-old tree.

Seasonal rhythm

Pre-emergent by late February (crabgrass germinates early here), feed after full green-up in April, survive July and August on your allowed days plus hand watering, then use the long mild fall for planting, aeration, and any hardscape work. Winter is for oak pruning and irrigation repairs.
